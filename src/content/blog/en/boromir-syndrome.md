In February 2026, U.S. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth delivered an ultimatum to Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei: remove all safety guardrails by 5:01 PM Friday, or be designated a supply chain risk.

When I read this, what came to mind was not a tech headline but a scene from an old fantasy novel.

## The Council of Elrond

In The Lord of the Rings, the Council of Elrond is the moment that determines the fate of Middle-earth. Representatives of every free people gather to decide what to do with the One Ring. And there, Boromir rises and speaks.

*"It is a gift. A gift to the foes of Mordor."*

Boromir's logic is simple and powerful. Gondor stands on the front lines against Mordor. The enemy is mighty and the threat is real. We need a stronger weapon. Here lies the One Ring, a power of immeasurable force — to destroy it would be strategic suicide.

From a soldier's perspective, from a commander waging war, this logic seems flawless.

## The Pentagon's Logic

The Pentagon of February 2026 deploys the exact same reasoning.

China is surging ahead in the AI arms race. The security threats are real. We need AI without restrictions. Bans on mass surveillance? Limits on autonomous weapons? These safeguards are tying our hands. What does one company's ethical stubbornness matter in the face of national security?

Hegseth's January memo was unambiguous: the Department of Defense would only use AI models "free from usage policy constraints that may limit lawful military applications."

Where Boromir proposed to *wield* the Ring, the Pentagon proposes to *remove all guardrails* from AI. Same logic, same conviction, same danger.

## What the Ring Corrupts

What Tolkien showed us through Boromir is not simple villainy. Boromir is a good man. He loves Gondor and wants to protect his people. There is no selfishness in his motives.

And that is precisely the point. **The true danger of the One Ring is not that it tempts the wicked, but that it operates through the good intentions of the virtuous.**

Boromir did not want the Ring for personal power. He wanted it to save his country. But the Ring corrupts every wielder regardless of how noble their purpose, because the Ring's essential nature is domination and control.

Mass surveillance technology works the same way. It begins with the purpose of finding terrorists, but once the tool exists, its scope of use expands relentlessly — to monitoring political dissidents, to civilian control, and ultimately to the erosion of democracy itself. Because the essential nature of the tool is surveillance and control.

## Faramir's Choice

Tolkien created a counterpart to Boromir: his brother Faramir.

Faramir loves Gondor too. He knows the reality of war. But when he encounters the One Ring, he says:

*"I would not take this thing, if it lay by the highway."*

Faramir's refusal is not because the threat is unreal. It is because he understands that wielding such power is itself the path to destroying what you seek to protect.

This is precisely the choice Dario Amodei has made. Amodei does not deny that AI can contribute to national security. Anthropic had already signed a $200 million contract with the DOD and was integrating AI into classified networks. What he refused were exactly two things — mass warrantless surveillance of American citizens and fully autonomous weapons that select and engage targets without meaningful human oversight.

In a conversation with New York Times columnist Ross Douthat, Amodei had already warned that AI could render public surveillance "hyperlegible." A world where every citizen's every action is tracked, analyzed, and patterned. That is not security. That is control.

## The Boromir Syndrome

I want to call this **the Boromir Syndrome**.

The temptation to use dangerous power for good ends. A temptation made more persuasive by the reality of the threat. And the self-assurance that this power can be "controlled."

The core symptom of the Boromir Syndrome is this: the belief that the reality of a threat justifies the danger of the means. The enemy is strong, so we must use any weapon available. Within this logic, every safeguard becomes weakness, and every limitation becomes an advantage handed to the enemy.

It is no coincidence that the Pentagon called Amodei a "liar" with a "God complex." Boromir said the same of those who proposed to destroy the Ring — that they were fools, cowards, or allies of the enemy.

But anyone who has read The Lord of the Rings to the end knows: Boromir's way could never have defeated Sauron. The moment you use the Ring, you are already playing the game Sauron wants you to play.

## The Question of Our Time

The Anthropic-Pentagon dispute is not a simple contract disagreement between a corporation and a government. This is the Council of Elrond of our age.

On one side stands a real security threat; on the other, a surveillance infrastructure that, once unleashed, is nearly impossible to reverse. On one side stands the urgency of not falling behind in an arms race; on the other, the foundational principles of democracy.

That Sam Altman sent a memo to OpenAI employees supporting the same position as Anthropic, that over a hundred Google employees sent letters demanding similar restrictions — this is Middle-earth's free peoples beginning, one by one, to recognize the danger of the Ring.

The One Ring was ultimately destroyed in the fires of Mount Doom. That it was the only way — the wise in the story knew from the beginning.

Before the One Ring of AI, will we be Boromir, or will we be Faramir? That choice will define this era.
